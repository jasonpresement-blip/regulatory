From Slavery to Freedom
Chapter I

A People in Chains

Over 3,000 years ago, the Jewish people were slaves in Egypt under Pharaoh Ramesses II — building cities, hauling stone, no rest, no rights. Fearing the growing Hebrew population, Ramesses ordered every newborn Hebrew boy killed.

One mother refused. She wove a papyrus basket, waterproofed it, placed her baby inside, and set him adrift on the Nile. Pharaoh's own daughter found him while bathing in the river, took him in, and raised him as her own. She named him Moses — meaning "drawn from the water."

Chapter II

A Voice in the Fire

Moses grew up in the palace alongside Egyptian royalty. But when he discovered his true identity and witnessed the brutality his people endured, something broke in him. He killed an Egyptian overseer who was beating a Hebrew slave, then fled into the desert.

There, at a burning bush that somehow wouldn't burn out, God gave him his mission: go back to Egypt, stand in front of the most powerful man in the world, and tell him to let the Hebrews go. Moses, understandably nervous, asked his brother Aaron to come with him.

Let my people go.

Chapter III

Ten Plagues Upon Egypt

Pharaoh said no. Repeatedly. So God sent ten plagues — and after each one, Ramesses would almost budge, then didn't. The man was stubborn in a way that cost his country enormously.

The tenth plague finally broke him. God had told Moses to instruct every Hebrew family to mark their doorposts with lamb's blood, so the angel of death would recognize the home and pass over it. That is exactly where the name comes from.

Chapter IV

The Night of Departure

When the plague struck and hit Ramesses' own household, he summoned Moses in the middle of the night. "Go," he said. "Take your people and leave."

They left so fast their bread dough never had time to rise. They baked it flat on their backs in the desert sun — that unleavened bread, matzah, is why Jews avoid regular bread entirely for eight days every Passover.

Ramesses, his grief cooling into rage, changed his mind and sent six hundred chariots after them.

— 4 —
Chapter V

The Sea Splits Open

The Hebrews reached the Red Sea with an army closing in behind them. Moses raised his staff, and the sea split into two great walls of water. They crossed on dry land.

When the Egyptian army charged in after them, the waters came crashing back down. That was the end of that.

Four hundred years of slavery. Over in a night.

The Legacy

A Story Retold Every Year

On the far shore, Miriam — Moses' sister, who had watched over him as a baby in the Nile — took up a tambourine and led the women in song and dance. After 400 years of bondage, the Jewish people were free.

Passover is celebrated each spring, usually falling in March or April. Families gather for a ritual meal called a Seder, built around a text called the Haggadah that walks through the whole story. The central idea isn't just historical — it's that every generation should experience the Exodus as if it happened to them personally.
